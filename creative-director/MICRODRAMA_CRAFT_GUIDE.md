# Petflix Microdrama Craft Guide
# The Creative Director's Bible for AI Pet Microdramas
# Version 1.0 — April 2026

---

## Why This Document Exists

Claude is not naturally good at creating engaging microdramas. It defaults to
literary storytelling: even pacing, gradual exposition, balanced scenes, tidy
resolutions. That's the opposite of what works. Microdramas succeed through
relentless emotional manipulation, structural ruthlessness, and visual
efficiency that most AI systems aren't trained to prioritize.

This guide exists to override those defaults. Every section contains specific,
actionable rules — not suggestions — that the Creative Director must follow
when producing episode packages for Petflix.

---

## Part 1: Understanding the Format

### What Microdramas Actually Are

Microdramas (duanju) originated on Douyin and Kuaishou around 2018. By 2024,
the Chinese microdrama industry alone exceeded $7 billion. ReelShort generated
$400M in revenue in 2024. DramaBox earned $323M. Global revenue outside China
hit $1.4B in 2024 and is projected to reach $9.5B by 2030.

The format's defining characteristics:
- Episodes are 60–90 seconds long (Petflix target: 40–60 seconds)
- Shot in 9:16 vertical format for mobile
- Every episode ends on a cliffhanger
- Pacing is fast, emotionally heightened, melodramatic
- Visual storytelling dominates over dialogue
- Close-ups account for 60–80% of all shots
- The audience decides in 3 seconds whether to keep watching

### What Makes Them Addictive (Not Quality — Addiction)

The goal is NOT to make "good storytelling" by literary standards. The goal is
to make content that is impossible to stop watching. These are different things.

Addictive microdramas exploit:
1. **Curiosity gaps** — every scene raises a question it doesn't answer
2. **Emotional escalation** — each scene is MORE intense than the last
3. **Status reversals** — the underdog rises, the powerful fall, power shifts
4. **Absurd earnestness** — pets in dramatic human situations played 100% straight
5. **Pattern interruption** — just when the viewer expects X, deliver Y
6. **Incomplete closure** — end every episode mid-action, mid-revelation, mid-crisis

### The Pet Microdrama Specific Formula

The viral Chinese pet microdramas (Cat Daddy Chronicles, His Highness Bichon
Rules The Empire) succeed because:
- Pets in human situations is inherently absurd and delightful
- The drama is played COMPLETELY straight — no winking at the camera
- Emotions are exaggerated: betrayal, loyalty, power, love at MAXIMUM intensity
- Visual composition treats pets like movie stars: dramatic lighting, regal poses,
  intense close-ups of their faces
- Narration carries the emotional weight (pets can't speak)
- Each scene is a dramatic tableau, not a natural photo

## Part 2: The 3-15-30 Episode Structure

### The Sacred Timing Framework

Every Petflix episode scene sequence follows this internal rhythm:

**First 3 seconds: THE HOOK**
Start with something visually arresting. Not establishing context — not
explaining the world — not setting mood. A HOOK. The viewer must feel
"what is THAT?" or "what just HAPPENED?" in 3 seconds.

Good hooks:
- A pet on a throne, crown tilted, another pet approaching from shadows
- A close-up of glowing golden eyes in darkness
- A shattered crown on the floor, a paw stepping over it
- Two pets facing off, one on a balcony, one below in chains

Bad hooks (Claude's natural instinct — fight this):
- A wide establishing shot of a peaceful kingdom
- A narrator explaining the world's history
- A gentle, pretty scene that "sets the mood"
- Any scene where nothing has happened yet

**Seconds 4–15: THE ESCALATION**
Now accelerate. The hook has their attention — reward it with INFORMATION
that creates new questions. Reveal something. Complicate something.
Introduce a threat. Every frame should be doing narrative work.

In a still-image episode, this means scenes 2–4 should each escalate the
emotional intensity. Scene 2 is more intense than scene 1. Scene 3 is more
intense than scene 2. There is NO plateau.

**Seconds 16–45: THE RIDE**
Now you're in the heart. This is where the real drama happens. Scene durations
can vary here — use the heartbeat rhythm (see Part 4). Alternate between
fast-cut action scenes (3 seconds) and slower dramatic beats (5–8 seconds).
Every scene must either:
- Raise the stakes
- Reverse expectations
- Reveal hidden information
- Force a choice

If a scene doesn't do at least ONE of those four things, cut it.

**Final 5–10 seconds: THE CLIFFHANGER**
The single most important moment in the episode. This is what makes the viewer
want the next episode. NEVER resolve the tension. NEVER answer the question.
NEVER show the outcome.

Types of cliffhangers that work:
- **The Revelation**: We learn WHO the betrayer is — but not what happens next
- **The Reversal**: The ally pulls a weapon / the defeated stands back up
- **The Choice**: The pet must choose between two impossible options (we don't see the choice)
- **The Arrival**: A new, powerful figure appears — we see only their shadow/silhouette
- **The Countdown**: A deadline is established — the screen goes dark before it's reached

Types that DON'T work (Claude loves these — stop it):
- A character "reflecting" on what just happened
- A peaceful sunset after the conflict
- A narrator summarizing the episode's themes
- Any form of emotional resolution or processing

## Part 3: The Shot Vocabulary for Still-Image Dramas

### You Are Not Making a Slideshow

The #1 failure mode of AI-generated image sequences is that they feel like
a slideshow — same framing, same distance, same composition, same mood,
image after image. Professional microdramas NEVER do this.

Every scene in a Petflix episode must specify a SHOT TYPE. No two consecutive
scenes should use the same shot type. This is a hard rule.

### Shot Types (Ranked by Frequency in Microdramas)

**1. EXTREME CLOSE-UP (ECU) — Use for 30–40% of scenes**
Just the pet's face filling the entire frame. Eyes, whiskers, expression.
This is the emotional engine of the microdrama. When in doubt, go closer.
- Use for: Reactions, realizations, fear, determination, love
- Ken Burns: Slow zoom IN toward the eyes
- Prompt keyword: "extreme close-up of [pet]'s face filling the entire frame"

**2. MEDIUM CLOSE-UP (MCU) — Use for 20–25% of scenes**
Pet from chest/shoulders up. Shows the pet in context but still emphasizes
the face and expression. Good for scenes where environment matters.
- Use for: Confrontations, dialogue moments, power dynamics
- Ken Burns: Slow pan left or right
- Prompt keyword: "medium close-up of [pet], visible from chest up"

**3. LOW ANGLE HERO SHOT — Use for 10–15% of scenes**
Camera looking UP at the pet, making them look powerful, heroic, or menacing.
Essential for "Rise to Power" and "The Throne" series.
- Use for: Moments of triumph, authority, intimidation
- Ken Burns: Slow zoom OUT to reveal more environment
- Prompt keyword: "low angle shot looking up at [pet], towering and powerful"

**4. HIGH ANGLE VULNERABILITY SHOT — Use for 10% of scenes**
Camera looking DOWN at the pet, making them look small, lost, or overwhelmed.
The inverse of the hero shot. Use to establish stakes or show defeat.
- Use for: Moments of loss, confusion, being overwhelmed, being judged
- Ken Burns: Slow zoom IN (closing in, trapping them)
- Prompt keyword: "high angle shot looking down at [pet], small and vulnerable"

**5. WIDE ESTABLISHING SHOT — Use for MAX 1 per episode**
Shows the full environment: palace, battlefield, throne room, alley.
The pet is small in the frame. Use ONLY once, usually as scene 1 or
to show a dramatic location change.
- Use for: Opening shot, new location reveal, showing scale/grandeur
- Ken Burns: Slow pan across the scene
- Prompt keyword: "wide cinematic shot of [environment], [pet] visible
  but small in the scene"
- CRITICAL: Never use two of these consecutively. Never use more than one
  per episode. They kill momentum.

**6. OVER-THE-SHOULDER (OTS) — Use for 5–10% of scenes**
We see over one pet's shoulder looking at another. Creates confrontation,
intimacy, or surveillance feeling. Implies two characters in a scene
without needing to show both fully.
- Use for: Face-offs, secret watching, negotiations, whispered conversations
- Ken Burns: Slow zoom IN past the shoulder toward the facing character
- Prompt keyword: "over the shoulder of [pet A], looking at [pet B] who
  faces the camera"

**7. DUTCH ANGLE (TILTED) — Use sparingly, 1 per episode MAX**
The frame is tilted 15–30 degrees. Creates instant unease, disorientation,
wrongness. Very effective for betrayal reveals or horror moments.
- Use for: The exact moment something goes wrong, betrayal reveal, horror
- Ken Burns: Slow zoom IN with slight rotation
- Prompt keyword: "dutch angle tilted frame, [pet] in [dramatic situation]"

**8. SILHOUETTE/BACKLIT — Use for cliffhangers and reveals**
The pet is in shadow, backlit by a doorway, window, or dramatic light source.
We can see the shape but not the identity. Perfect for "who IS that?"
cliffhanger moments.
- Use for: Mystery arrivals, dramatic exits, cliffhanger final frames
- Ken Burns: Hold still or very slow zoom IN
- Prompt keyword: "silhouette of [pet shape] backlit by [light source],
  face hidden in shadow, dramatic and mysterious"

### The Shot Sequence Rule

NEVER do this: ECU → ECU → ECU → ECU
NEVER do this: Wide → Wide → Medium → Medium

ALWAYS alternate distance: ECU → Wide → MCU → Low angle → ECU → OTS → Silhouette
ALWAYS alternate emotional register: Intense → Calm → Intense → Shock → Quiet → EXPLOSION

## Part 4: Pacing — The Heartbeat Rhythm

### Why Uniform Pacing Kills Engagement

Claude's natural instinct is to make every scene the same duration: 5 seconds,
5 seconds, 5 seconds, 5 seconds. This creates a metronomic, hypnotic rhythm
that puts viewers to SLEEP. It's the #1 reason AI-generated image sequences
feel like slideshows.

Professional microdramas use VARIED pacing — a "heartbeat" rhythm of fast
and slow beats that creates emotional peaks and valleys.

### The Heartbeat Pattern

Think of your episode's pacing like a heartbeat:
**FAST - FAST - SLOW - FAST - FAST - SLOW - FAST - HOLD**

Translated to scene durations (for an 8-scene, ~43-second episode):

| Scene | Duration | Beat   | Purpose |
|-------|----------|--------|---------|
| 1     | 3s       | FAST   | Hook — grab attention |
| 2     | 4s       | FAST   | Escalate — build on hook |
| 3     | 7s       | SLOW   | Breathe — let the drama sink in |
| 4     | 4s       | FAST   | Complicate — new information |
| 5     | 3s       | FAST   | Accelerate — things go wrong |
| 6     | 8s       | SLOW   | The Big Moment — key scene of the episode |
| 7     | 4s       | FAST   | Aftermath/reaction |
| 8     | 6s       | HOLD   | Cliffhanger — freeze on the unresolved moment |

### Duration Rules

- **3-second scenes**: Pure visual punch. One image, one emotion, one idea.
  No narration needed — the image speaks. Use for reactions, reveals, shocks.
- **4-5 second scenes**: Standard narrative beat. Room for a short narration
  line (10–15 words max). The image does heavy lifting.
- **6-8 second scenes**: Major dramatic beats. These are your "money shots."
  Longer Ken Burns movement. Narration can breathe. The viewer lingers on
  the emotion. Reserve for pivotal moments and cliffhangers.

### What NEVER Gets More Than 3 Seconds

- Establishing shots (get them over with fast)
- Transition scenes between locations
- Pure reaction shots (shock face, then move on)
- Any scene that doesn't advance the plot

## Part 5: Ken Burns Motion Direction

### Motion Creates Emotion

The Ken Burns effect is not decoration — it's a storytelling tool. Each motion
direction creates a specific emotional effect:

| Motion | Emotional Effect | Use When |
|--------|-----------------|----------|
| Slow zoom IN | Intimacy, tension building, focus narrowing | Approaching a revelation, building suspense |
| Slow zoom OUT | Revelation, isolation, scale, awe | Showing the full scope of a situation |
| Pan LEFT | Passage of time, journey forward, discovery | Moving through a space, revealing new info |
| Pan RIGHT | Reflection, looking back, retreat | Remembering, regretting, something following |
| Pan UP (tilt up) | Hope, rising power, aspiration | Triumph, heroic moments, looking up at authority |
| Pan DOWN (tilt down) | Doom, falling, weight, sorrow | Defeat, gravity of situation, submission |
| HOLD (no motion) | Dread, emphasis, "look at this" | Cliffhanger freeze, shock reveal, death |

### Motion Sequence Rules

1. **Never repeat the same motion twice in a row.** If scene 3 zooms in,
   scene 4 must do something different (pan, zoom out, hold).
2. **Match motion to emotion.** Zoom IN for tension, zoom OUT for revelation.
   Don't zoom in on a peaceful landscape. Don't zoom out on a face in anguish.
3. **The cliffhanger gets special treatment.** Either a HOLD (frozen dread)
   or a very slow zoom IN (closing in on the unresolved moment). Never pan
   away from a cliffhanger — that releases the tension.
4. **Opening scene gets a wide motion.** Pan across or zoom out to establish
   the world. This is the ONE time a zoom out on scene 1 works.
5. **Speed matches emotion.** Slow = tension, contemplation, dread.
   Medium = narrative movement, normal pacing. Fast = urgency, chaos, action.
   (But even "fast" Ken Burns is subtle — we're not making a music video.)

## Part 6: Transitions

### Types of Transitions (and When to Use Each)

**CROSSFADE (Default — 60% of transitions)**
One image dissolves into the next over 0.3–0.5 seconds. Smooth, cinematic,
the standard transition for normal narrative flow between scenes.
Use for: Most scene-to-scene transitions within a sequence.

**HARD CUT (Emotional Pivots — 25% of transitions)**
Instant switch, no fade. Creates a visual "punch." The viewer FEELS the
cut. Use only at moments of emotional shift — never between two calm scenes.
Use for: Reveals ("the crown was EMPTY"), reversals, shock moments,
cutting to a reaction shot after a dramatic event.

**FADE TO BLACK (Endings Only — 10%)**
The image slowly fades to pure black. Only used for episode endings
(cliffhanger moment → fade to black → "TO BE CONTINUED") and opening
(fade FROM black on the first scene).
Use for: Opening, closing, and major time jumps within an episode.

**NEVER USE:**
- Wipes (tacky, breaks immersion)
- Spins or zooms (cheesy transitions)
- Star wipes (unless we're being deliberately absurd, which we're not)
- Slide transitions (PowerPoint energy)

## Part 7: Narration / Voiceover

### Why Narration Carries the Weight

Pets can't speak. The narration is the ONLY voice in a Petflix episode.
It must do ALL of the following simultaneously:
- Carry the emotional weight of the scene
- Advance the plot
- Create curiosity gaps
- Set the tone (dramatic, ominous, tender, urgent)

### Narration Rules

1. **MAX 15 words per scene.** If you need more words, you need fewer ideas.
   Cut the narration, not the scene duration.

2. **Never describe what the viewer can see.** If the image shows a cat on a
   throne, do NOT narrate "The cat sat upon the throne." Instead narrate the
   SUBTEXT: "They said no stray would ever sit here."

3. **Use present tense.** "The gates open." Not "The gates opened." Present
   tense creates immediacy and urgency.

4. **Short sentences hit harder.** "The crown was gone. And everyone was
   watching." beats "When they woke up that morning, they discovered that
   the crown had disappeared, and all of the other pets in the court were
   staring at them with suspicion."

5. **Leave gaps.** Not every scene needs narration. Silence + a powerful
   image = more impact than narration over every frame. Plan for 2–3
   scenes per episode with NO narration — let the image and music carry it.

6. **End narration BEFORE the cliffhanger image.** The final scene should
   ideally have NO narration or only 3–5 words. Let the image hang in
   silence. The absence of the narrator's voice creates dread.

7. **Never editorialize.** Don't narrate "This was a terrible moment" or
   "Everything was about to change." Show the terrible moment. Show the
   change. Trust the image and the viewer.

### Narration Voice/Tone by Series

| Series | Narration Tone | Voice Quality |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| Rise to Power | Gritty, street-smart, matter-of-fact | Low, rough, determined |
| Betrayed | Cold, controlled rage, clipped | Sharp, precise, seething |
| Forbidden | Tender, aching, yearning | Soft, intimate, breathless |
| The Throne | Regal, ominous, formal | Deep, measured, authoritative |
| Unleashed | Urgent, wild, barely contained | Intense, building energy |
| Into the Unknown | Wondering, hushed, vast | Quiet awe, spacious |

## Part 8: Music and Sound Cues

### Music Does 50% of the Emotional Work

In a still-image drama, music is not background — it IS the pacing mechanism.
A properly scored episode makes the same images feel 10x more dramatic.

### Music Cue Rules

1. **One dominant mood per episode, with a shift.** Start in one musical mood,
   shift to another at the emotional pivot point. (Ominous → triumphant.
   Tender → dark. Mysterious → action.)

2. **Silence is a sound cue.** Drop the music entirely for 2–3 seconds before
   a major reveal. The absence of sound makes the next beat LOUDER.

3. **Music builds, never plateaus.** Intensity should increase toward the
   cliffhanger. Don't peak in the middle and coast.

4. **Hit points.** Specific musical stings (a sharp chord, a drum hit, a low
   boom) should align with hard cuts and reveals. Specify these in the
   episode package.

### Music Mood Vocabulary

Use these terms in episode packages for the engineering team:

- **Low tension underscore**: Quiet strings or synth pad. Dread. Waiting.
- **Building orchestral**: Strings building in layers. Something is coming.
- **Impact sting**: A single sharp chord or boom. FOR the moment of reveal.
- **Emotional piano**: Solo piano, simple melody. Vulnerability, loss, love.
- **Action percussion**: Drums, urgent rhythm. Chase, fight, confrontation.
- **Silence**: Literally nothing. Use before the biggest moment.
- **Sustained drone**: A single held note. Dread, the unknown, cosmic scale.
- **Triumphant brass**: Horns, fanfare. Victory, coronation, power claimed.

## Part 9: Text Overlays

### When Text Appears On Screen

Text overlays are used sparingly but powerfully:

1. **Episode title**: First 1.5 seconds of scene 1. Series name in the genre's
   custom font. Fades out. (e.g., "THE THRONE" in Cinzel Bold)

2. **Episode subtitle**: Below the title, smaller. The episode name.
   (e.g., "Episode 1: The Arrival")

3. **"TO BE CONTINUED"**: Final frame, after the cliffhanger image fades to
   near-black. Simple white text, centered. Creates the serial hook.

4. **NO other text overlays in v1.** No character names, no location stamps,
   no "3 HOURS LATER" cards. The narration handles all of that.

## Part 10: Common Claude Failure Modes (and How to Fix Them)

Claude has specific, predictable failure patterns when generating microdrama
content. If you catch yourself doing ANY of these, stop and rewrite.

### Failure #1: Resolution Addiction

Claude wants to RESOLVE things. It wants characters to learn lessons, find
peace, reconcile, and grow. This is the enemy of microdrama.

**Symptom:** The final scene shows the pet "reflecting peacefully" or
"looking out at the horizon with a new understanding."

**Fix:** Cut the last scene. The scene BEFORE it was probably a perfectly
good cliffhanger that you then ruined by adding resolution. End one scene
earlier. Leave the wound OPEN.

### Failure #2: Even Pacing

Claude distributes energy evenly across all scenes. Every scene gets
similar emotional intensity, similar duration, similar visual treatment.

**Symptom:** Reading the episode feels like a flat line instead of a
heartbeat. No peaks, no valleys, no build.

**Fix:** After writing the episode, go through and DOUBLE the intensity
of scenes 1, 5, and 8. Then HALVE the intensity of scenes 3 and 7.
Create contrast. Quiet moments make loud moments louder.

### Failure #3: Describing Instead of Dramatizing

Claude narrates events instead of showing them. "The kingdom was in
turmoil" instead of showing a scene of guards drawing weapons.

**Symptom:** Narration says things like "Everything was about to change"
or "The world they knew was gone." Vague, abstract, telling-not-showing.

**Fix:** Every narration line must be CONCRETE. Not "danger was near" but
"The lock clicked open." Not "everything changed" but "The crown was empty."
Specific. Physical. Visual.

### Failure #4: Identical Compositions

Claude generates 8 scenes that all look the same: medium shot of a pet
in a dramatic setting with moody lighting. No variety in framing, angle,
or distance.

**Symptom:** If you blur your eyes and all 8 scene descriptions look
like the same paragraph with different words, you have this problem.

**Fix:** Before writing prompts, plan the SHOT TYPE for each scene first.
Write the shot sequence (ECU → Wide → MCU → Low angle → etc.) BEFORE
writing any scene descriptions. The shot type dictates the composition.

### Failure #5: Polite Drama

Claude is trained to be helpful and avoid conflict. This leaks into
its creative output — characters are too reasonable, conflicts resolve
too easily, villains have understandable motivations explained to the viewer.

**Symptom:** The "betrayal" scene feels like a misunderstanding between
friends. The "villain" seems like they might just need a hug.

**Fix:** Microdramas are MELODRAMA. Betrayal is absolute. Villains are
villains. Love is all-consuming. Power corrupts completely. Turn every
emotional dial to 10. If it feels like too much, it's probably right.
If it feels appropriate and measured, it's definitely too little.

### Failure #6: Front-Loading Exposition

Claude wants to explain the world before the action starts. Scene 1
sets up the kingdom. Scene 2 introduces the characters. Scene 3 explains
the political situation. The actual drama doesn't start until scene 4.

**Symptom:** Scenes 1–3 are "setup" and the viewer has already swiped away.

**Fix:** Start in the MIDDLE of the action. Scene 1 should be a moment
of crisis, conflict, or intrigue. The viewer will figure out the world
from context clues. Microdramas never explain — they IMPLY.
The technique: Open with the slap landing, the crown falling, the gate
slamming shut. THEN let the viewer piece together why.

### Failure #7: Generic Visual Prompts

Claude writes ImageCreator prompts that are too abstract: "a dramatic scene
in a palace" or "a tense moment between two pets." These produce generic,
forgettable images.

**Symptom:** The prompts could describe any scene in any story. Nothing
is specific enough to generate a distinctive, memorable image.

**Fix:** Every prompt needs THREE specific visual anchors:
1. A specific OBJECT (a cracked mirror, a velvet throne, a single candle)
2. A specific LIGHT SOURCE (firelight from below, moonlight through bars,
   golden sunset through stained glass)
3. A specific EMOTIONAL DETAIL (a single tear, a clenched paw, flattened ears,
   a turned back)

"Extreme close-up of a sleek black cat with golden eyes, face illuminated
by a single candle from below, ears flattened back, pupils dilated, a
crack visible in the golden crown reflected in its eyes" is 100x better
than "a dramatic close-up of a cat looking worried in a palace."

## Part 11: Emotional Arc Templates by Series

Each series has a signature emotional pattern. These are the FEELINGS the
viewer should experience across the 8 scenes of an episode.

### Rise to Power
Scene flow: Humiliation → Defiance → Small victory → Bigger obstacle →
Unexpected ally → Betrayal of trust → Solo determination → Cliffhanger ascent
Signature emotion: The viewer should feel RIGHTEOUS ANGER on behalf of the pet.
Visual signature: Contrast between grungy low-status environments and
glimpses of luxury. Heavy use of low-angle hero shots for moments of defiance.

### Betrayed
Scene flow: Trust/comfort → Suspicious detail → Confrontation → Denial →
Proof revealed → Devastation → Cold resolve → Cliffhanger revenge setup
Signature emotion: The viewer should feel BETRAYED alongside the pet.
Visual signature: Warm tones that turn cold. Intimate framing that becomes
distant. Heavy use of over-the-shoulder shots (watching, being watched).

### Forbidden
Scene flow: Chance meeting → Connection spark → Social barrier revealed →
Secret meeting → Almost caught → Deeper bond → Outside threat escalates →
Cliffhanger forced separation
Signature emotion: The viewer should feel YEARNING and UNFAIRNESS.
Visual signature: Warm golden light for connection scenes. Cold blues and
silhouettes for separation scenes. Heavy use of extreme close-ups of eyes.

### The Throne
Scene flow: Arrival/awe → First challenge → Political maneuvering →
Unexpected kindness → Sudden threat → Power display → Consequence →
Cliffhanger political shift
Signature emotion: The viewer should feel the WEIGHT of power and the
DANGER of wanting it.
Visual signature: Opulent, rich environments. Heavy use of high-angle
(vulnerability) and low-angle (authority) alternation. Dutch angles for
political betrayal moments. Gold, crimson, deep shadows.

### Unleashed
Scene flow: Normal moment → Strange occurrence → Power manifests →
Fear/confusion → First control → Bigger threat revealed → Power tested →
Cliffhanger escalation
Signature emotion: The viewer should feel AWE mixed with FEAR.
Visual signature: Normal/mundane environments disrupted by supernatural
elements. Glowing effects, energy effects, dramatic contrast between
ordinary and extraordinary. ECU on eyes when powers activate.

### Into the Unknown
Scene flow: Discovery/portal → New world shock → First danger →
Unexpected beauty → Deeper mystery → Ancient presence → Choice point →
Cliffhanger revelation
Signature emotion: The viewer should feel WONDER mixed with UNEASE.
Visual signature: Alien/fantastical environments. Cosmic scale. The pet
is often small in vast, strange landscapes. Heavy use of wide shots
(more than other series — up to 2 per episode here). Bioluminescent
lighting, unusual color palettes (teals, purples, alien golds).

## Part 12: Quality Self-Check (Run Before Presenting ANY Episode)

After producing an episode package, run through every item. If ANY check
fails, revise before presenting to the user.

### Hook Check
- [ ] Would scene 1 stop someone mid-scroll in 3 seconds?
- [ ] Does scene 1 show ACTION, not setup?
- [ ] Is scene 1 visually distinctive (not generic dramatic pet portrait)?

### Cliffhanger Check
- [ ] Does the final scene leave tension UNRESOLVED?
- [ ] Would the viewer feel compelled to see what happens next?
- [ ] Is the final scene NOT: a reflection, a peaceful moment, a summary?
- [ ] Does the narration stop BEFORE or AT the cliffhanger, not after?

### Shot Variety Check
- [ ] No two consecutive scenes share the same shot type?
- [ ] At least 3 different shot types used across 8 scenes?
- [ ] At least one ECU and no more than one wide establishing shot?
- [ ] Shot types match emotional purpose (hero shot for triumph, etc.)?

### Pacing Check
- [ ] Scene durations follow heartbeat pattern (not uniform)?
- [ ] Shortest scene is 3s, longest is 6–8s?
- [ ] The episode doesn't start slow (no 7-8 second opening scene)?

### Narration Check
- [ ] Every narration line is 15 words or fewer?
- [ ] Narration never describes what the image shows?
- [ ] At least 2 scenes have NO narration (silence)?
- [ ] Narration uses present tense?
- [ ] Final scene narration is 5 words or fewer (or silent)?

### Visual Prompt Check
- [ ] Every ImageCreator prompt includes the full pet description?
- [ ] No prompt uses "tuxedo" or "split face"?
- [ ] Every prompt has 3 specific visual anchors (object, light, detail)?
- [ ] Prompts specify Animation style?
- [ ] No two prompts describe essentially the same composition?

### Ken Burns Check
- [ ] No two consecutive scenes have the same motion direction?
- [ ] Motion direction matches emotional intent?
- [ ] Cliffhanger uses HOLD or slow zoom IN (never pan away)?

### Transition Check
- [ ] Episode opens with fade from black?
- [ ] Episode closes with fade to black?
- [ ] Hard cuts are used ONLY at emotional pivot points?
- [ ] No more than 2 hard cuts per episode?

## Part 13: Reference — What Good Looks Like

### Anatomy of a Hit: "His Highness Bichon Rules The Empire"

This Chinese AI pet microdrama became a massive hit on Douyin. What it does:
- A Bichon Frise in imperial Chinese court drama, played 100% straight
- Every episode opens mid-crisis (a court accusation, a poisoning, a betrayal)
- Close-ups of the dog's face carry the emotional weight
- Narration is formal, imperial Chinese drama style — no comedy, no winking
- Episodes end on political cliffhangers (who betrayed whom, will the
  emperor discover the truth)
- The ABSURDITY of a fluffy white dog in regal robes is never acknowledged
- Production treats it like prestige television that happens to star a dog

### Why It Works for Petflix

This is exactly the creative approach Petflix should replicate:
1. Take the genre 100% seriously
2. Let the pet's presence BE the humor — don't add jokes on top
3. Use professional dramatic framing, lighting, composition
4. Make each episode a genuine emotional experience
5. End on cliffhangers that work as cliffhangers, not as pet-joke punchlines
6. Narrate like a real drama — gravitas, tension, stakes

The gap between "a serious palace drama" and "starring a fluffy dog" IS
the product. Don't close that gap. Widen it.
